# Fence-Mounted Lumber Storage Rack
## Design & Build Guide

### Overview
A cantilever-style rack that mounts to your existing fence posts to store salvaged 2x6 boards off the ground during your backyard remodel.

---

## Design Specifications

### Key Features
- **Fence Mount**: Attaches directly to 6x6 fence posts (5ft spacing)
- **Ground Clearance**: 24" minimum (allows for yard grading)
- **Capacity**: 4 storage levels, each 12" apart
- **Reach**: 24" cantilever from fence
- **Materials**: Uses salvaged 2x6 boards
- **Hardware**: Standard deck screws

## Assembly Instructions

### Step 1: Prepare Vertical Brackets
1. Cut 2× 2x6 boards to 36" length
2. Mark mounting hole locations (4 holes per bracket):
   - 6" from bottom
   - 12" from bottom
   - 18" from bottom
   - 24" from bottom
3. Drill 3/16" pilot holes on centerline

### Step 2: Mount Brackets to Fence Posts
1. Mark fence posts at 24" above ground
2. Hold bracket against post (flush with post face)
3. Drive 4× #10×3" deck screws through pilot holes into post
4. Repeat for second post

### Step 3: Attach Horizontal Arms
For each level (starting from bottom at 24"):

1. Position 2x6×24" arm perpendicular to bracket
2. Arm should be centered on 5.5" bracket width
3. Secure with 2× #10×2.5" screws per connection
4. Repeat on other bracket
5. Space levels 12" apart vertically

### Step 4: Reinforce (Optional)
- Add diagonal bracing if storing very heavy loads
- Use construction adhesive at joints
- Add mid-span support for boards >10ft

## Load Capacity

### Engineering Notes:
- **Per arm**: ~200 lbs (2x6 cantilever at 24")
- **Per level**: ~400 lbs (two arms)
- **Total rack**: ~1600 lbs (4 levels)

**Conservative estimate**: Stack of 15× 2x6×10ft boards weighs ~300 lbs
- Each level can hold 1-2 layers of boards safely

### Safety Factors:
- Cantilever loading creates moment stress
- Most stress at fence connection point
- 4 screws per bracket provides good shear resistance
- 6x6 fence posts provide solid anchor

## Installation Tips

### Before You Start:
1. ✓ Verify fence posts are solid (wiggle test)
2. ✓ Locate any electrical/plumbing near fence
3. ✓ Check that fence posts are plumb (vertical)
4. ✓ Measure exact post spacing (may vary ±2")

### Pro Tips:
- Use a level to ensure arms are horizontal
- Start from bottom level, work up
- Pre-drill all holes to prevent splitting
- Countersink screws slightly for flush fit
- Leave 1" gap between boards for drainage/airflow

### Modifications:
- **Wider spacing?** Add cross-bracing between posts
- **Taller?** Add more levels (keep lowest at 24"+ for grading)
- **Longer boards?** Extend to 3+ fence posts
- **Heavier loads?** Use 2x8 or 2x10 for arms

## Questions to Consider

1. **How many fence posts do you want to use?**
   - 2 posts = one 5ft section
   - 3 posts = two 5ft sections (10ft total)
   - 4 posts = three 5ft sections (15ft total)

2. **How high do you want to go?**
   - Current design: 4 levels (24" to 60" high)
   - Can add levels up to ~72" for taller folks

3. **Board sorting strategy?**
   - Bottom level: 5-8ft boards
   - Level 2: 8-10ft boards
   - Level 3: 10-12ft boards
   - Top level: 12-15ft boards

4. **Temporary or permanent?**
   - Temporary: Use screws only (easy removal)
   - Permanent: Add adhesive + more screws
